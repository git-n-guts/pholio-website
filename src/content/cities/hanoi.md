The capital has been the capital for over a thousand years — with a few interruptions. That continuity is visible in the street grid of the Old Quarter, where lanes named for their medieval guilds (Hàng Bạc for silver, Hàng Đồng for bronze, Hàng Mã for ceremonial paper) still broadly sell what their names suggest. The city hasn't been curated for visitors. That's the point.

Hanoi sits at 21° north latitude, which matters more than any guidebook category. The winters are genuinely cool — January averages 17°C — and a light jacket is not theatre. The street food changes seasonally in ways that Ho Chi Minh City's doesn't: bún chả disappears from some stalls in the heat, comes back in the cool. Phở here is clearer and more restrained than the southern version. Bún bò Nam Bộ is a dish that exists specifically in Hanoi and nowhere else, despite what the name implies.

Hoan Kiem Lake is small enough to circle on foot in twelve minutes, but it anchors the city's sense of itself. The Ngoc Son Temple on its northern island has been there in some form since the eighteenth century. The lake freezes the pace of everything around it. People sit on its low stone walls at 6am and again at 9pm. There's no obvious reason to do this — and that's why it works.

The Old Quarter is dense and loud and occasionally exhausting. It rewards walking rather than planning: turn into an unmarked alley and you'll find a workshop making lacquerware or a family cooking bánh cuốn on a cloth stretched over a steaming pot. The traffic pattern looks like chaos and functions like a distributed system — you cross by moving at a constant pace and trusting that the motorcycles will route around you. They will.

West Lake (Hồ Tây) gives the city its other register. The lake is large enough to lose sight of the far shore on hazy days. Truc Bach Lake, separated from West Lake by a narrow causeway, is where bún cá is served at tables facing the water. The neighborhood around it — Trúc Bạch — is quieter and less photographed than the Old Quarter, and worth an afternoon.

Hanoi is not an easy city to summarize. It changes depending on what you came looking for. Spend three days minimum. Five is better.

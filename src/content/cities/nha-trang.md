Nha Trang announces itself from the air. If your flight into Cam Ranh descends over the coast, you'll see the bay first — a clean crescent of water backed by a city that has been trading beach access for high-rises since the early 2000s. The mountains behind it don't let the city sprawl; it compresses. That compression is what makes Nha Trang feel busy even in low season.

The airport is 35km south of the city. A taxi from Cam Ranh costs 300,000–350,000 VND depending on your negotiation and whether the driver turns on the meter. Grab is cheaper but less reliable at odd hours. The public bus (route 18) runs every 30 minutes from 5:30am to 6pm and costs 50,000 VND — it drops you at the northern end of Trần Phú, the beachfront road that defines the city's shape.

## What Nha Trang is actually for

The bay is the draw. Hon Mun marine park, 12km offshore, has the clearest water in central Vietnam — visibility hits 20 meters in dry season. Dive shops cluster along Biệt Thự street; a two-tank dive costs 2.2–2.8 million VND including gear and lunch on the boat. The operators vary widely in safety standards; look for PADI certification and avoid the cheapest option.

Hon Tam island has the resort beach — white sand, clear water, and a golf course built into the hillside. The cable car from the mainland costs 600,000 VND round-trip. You can day-trip without staying at the resort, but the island's primary function is to separate you from your money in controlled increments.

The mainland beach runs along Trần Phú for 6km. It's public, free, and crowded after 4pm when tour groups finish their island hops. The sand is fine but not pristine — the city's drainage system empties into the sea at multiple points along the strip.

## Food worth knowing about

Bún cá is the local specialty — a fish-based broth with rice noodles, fried fish cakes, and herbs. The best versions use cá mối (lizardfish) or cá thu (mackerel). Prices range from 35,000–50,000 VND at street stalls. Notable spots: Bún Cá Nguyên Loan on Lê Thành Phương (open 6am–10am), and Bún Cá 109 on Tô Hiến Thành.

Bánh canh chả cá is thicker — a gelatinous broth with fish cake slices. It's heavier than bún cá, more common at breakfast. Try it at Bánh Canh Bà Thừa (66 Nguyễn Trãi), open since 1984.

Seafood at chợ Đầm market is priced by the kilogram and negotiated before cooking. The display tanks are theatrical — lobsters, crabs, prawns, and fish you can't identify. Ask for the price per kilogram before pointing at anything. A meal for two with lobster and beer runs 800,000–1.2 million VND if you negotiate well.

## When to go

February through August is dry season. Temperatures sit at 28–33°C, humidity is manageable, and the water is clear enough for diving. September through November is rainy season — not constant rain, but heavy showers that can flood the lower streets for an hour or two. December and January are transition months; you'll get a mix.

The city is busiest during Vietnamese holidays: Tet (late January/early February), Reunification Day (April 30–May 1), and National Day (September 2). Hotel prices double during these periods.

## Getting around

Taxis are metered and cheap. Mai Linh and Vinasun are the most reliable operators. A cross-town trip costs 40,000–70,000 VND. Motorbike taxis (Grab Bike or traditional xe ôm) cost half that but you'll arrive sweaty.

Renting a motorbike costs 120,000–150,000 VND per day. Traffic is lighter than Hanoi or Saigon, but the beachfront road has speed cameras and police checkpoints. Wear a helmet.
